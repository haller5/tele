The snow was white. Whiter than usual. It was blindingly white. So it seemed to Renard. It seemed blindingly white, that is what it seemed. The sun reared an ugly form against the shapely white clouds which were thin against the otherwise empty, darkly glass skies. From beyond them, Renard could just catch the light glint of something just outside the skies, just out of reach. But he saw it nonetheless. Behind him, his steps followed closely behind his feet, peeling themselves into the snow as large boot footprints. The small flakes and clumps of snow caught themselves, hung tightly, onto the fabric of his boots. He walked alongside Father Craner. Craner was similarly striding along the snow, with a similar height. Wandering alongside them, flickering, was the sun fallen, falling into the further edge of the highlands. The two men walked near arm in arm, a closeness the sun could not put asunder. The sun lit up its now insignificant spot as though it were only a flame from a match. It looked as though the lightest wind, the gentlest embrace of the surrounding wintry wind, would extinguish it.

“Oh, look over there!”

Renard looked over. There was a large, rotting wooden signpost which stuck erect from the pure, airy snow. Snowflakes gathered at the top of the signpost, which was carved as an arrow, and had calligraphy upon it.

St. George, it read. Above it there were three crows. No more. Their eyes were intentful.

“Saint George?” repeated Renard.

“Yes, Saint George. That was the name of the town, if I recall correctly…” He trailed off in thought as they approached the sign. The crows did not move. “Shoo! Shoo!” said Father Craner. “Leave the sign alone!”

They did not move.

“Father, leave them. They haven’t done anything. Not even a slight movement. Look at them.”

Craner watched the crows. Truly, they hardly appeared to be alive, were it not for the intentful gleam in their dark eyes.

“It’s not what they do or don’t do. I’m not bothered by them, not at all.” He looked at the crows. He took off his coat. Holding it up, he used it to ward off the crows. “They’re bad for crops.”

The crows fluttered off through the raining snow.

“The people don’t need crows,” he murmured discontentedly, putting on his coat.

“Crops? In the winter?”

“Upon the final frost.”

“When would that be?”

“I don’t know,” he said. “It’s April, so what then…? May?”

“I can hardly believe it, too.”

“What?” He looked over to Renard. Renard paid him no mind, but continued walking. Or rather, he waded through the snow.

“That it even snows this deep into Spring.”

Craner grumbled.

“You think so, right? I’ve never seen anywhere that it snows like this.”

“It’s the mountains.”

“Even still,” he said. “Well.”

He looked back at Craner, who hadn’t moved.

“Still, it’s not as though I’ve seen snow at all, really,” he continued. “Sure, I think, though I’m not sure, I think it’d snow when I was younger. Where I lived. With my parents, my grandmother. Only very rarely, however. And when it did, it was the lightest snow you’d ever seen. You’d go and make a snowman, and it’d really be mostly dirt.”

Craner made a move to his coat, trying to find something.

Renard stood, waiting for Craner.

“Saint George’s what’s it’s called,” he muttered. He pulled out a pink envelope. “They sent it here, see.” He raised up to show Renard. He had a quite quaint grin on his face. “They sent it here, take a look. Because,” he continued, opening it and unfolding the letter. “They don’t have any good roads or signs besides this one right here.” He vaguely gestured at the signpost.

One crow was back.

“Look, com’ere.”

Renard walked toward him.

“Look, it’s quite a cute letter. Written by the little girl. Her handwriting looks pretty.”

Renard took the letter. “It’s getting late.”

Father Craner’s grin dissolved. “Alright.”

“I’ll take a look at the letter.”

“Read it out loud, please.”

Renard read it out loud.

“Dear Father Craner. I met an old man in the village who’d told me he knew you—.”

“I don’t recall any such man.”

“Yeah. Alright,” he continued. “Who’d’ told me he knew you. I’ve never spoken to him. But the others— the adults— they knew him. So I trust him. He gave me your address. The post passes rather infrequently, and when it does it passes at the most inconvenient of moments. It has no schedule. We have no post boxes, either. So I entrusted the old man with this letter. He, since he’s always at home, will give it to the post man. I may be in school. If this letter to you bears any semblance of significance, please come to my village. It is really dire. And my mother. It’s my mother.”

“It is really weird, is it not?”

“How so?”

“Well, really there’s no information at all. And look, do you see the very end there.”

“Yeah…” He did not notice anything.

“Look, let me see.” The Father ran to catch up to him. “There,” he pointed. “At the very end there, it seems as though there’s another stroke. But she never continued it. She was going to write more.”

“More? Could have been just an accident.”

“An accident, that’s what I thought of, of course. But, I don’t think it’s an accident.”

“Why?”

“I don’t. There’s no reason for it.”

Renard ignored him.

“Well,” continued Craner. “That’s not it anyway. I find it strange she’d reach out to me.”

“Because of the old guy. She says it right there.” He hands the letter back to Craner.

“Well, sure. But I’ve no idea who this man is. I’ve never met anybody of the country here. They’re foreign. They hardly speak English. Only recently have they taught the little children English. I’d have no opportunity to meet an old man.”

“If he moved?”

“Sure, that could be. But to here?” He gestured at the large field of snow. “Regardless, she makes her mother out to be quite visibly sick. That’s not a theological problem. I’ve no matter here! I don’t know what they need me for!”

“Be that as it may, that’s why I’m here.”

“Yes. Yes, that’s right. I did invite you here.” They walked closer. “I just thought that—”

Just across from them, though still very distant, they could see the faint markings of a corn field. It was a very large corn field. The village was made of small dotted houses spreading out toward the North— right of the setting sun.

“That’s the village?”

“Yes, I’d believe so.”

They were both struck by the village. It was really seemingly abandoned. There was not the dot of a person in sight. It was approaching night, however— there would be no reason to be out. Still, the lamps the village seemed to have were so terribly dim that they hardly spilled from the homes’ interiors into the street, besides the faintest puddles of a flickering, incandescent light just hardly glowing from the windows. On the other hand, the sky was a nightish and full blue.

“You thought what?” asked Renard.

“I thought you’d be of some help.”

“I’m no theologian.”

“Right. You’re a psychiatrist. I thought you’d help.”

“Right.”

They walked down the steep snowy slopes, with their boots dragging across the landscape.

They reached the nearby stables. The horses, adorned with the specks of snow and wearing jewels of ice which clung to their fur, looked elegant in the wooden stables. They were a great array of colours. Black, brown, white. The black horses were especially beautiful. Upon their left, the little shafts of sunlight, casting quiet rays which floated above the ground, reached the corn fields. There were quaint scarecrows. There were no crows.

Not much later, as they meandered across the small village, did they find that the sun had completely sunk into the horizon. The moon was well in the sky, glowing. The stars were in complete view. They appeared to imitate the radiant snowflakes falling below, as the stars of the sky appeared like static flakes of snow in some way.

“Over here.”

“How do you know the address?” asked Renard.

“It says on the back of the letter.” Craner showed the letter. As he had said, there was writing on the back. A short address.

“What does an address matter in a town with no roads?”

There were indeed no roads.

There was little of anything.

They met an old woman. The woman had a strange eye. It didn’t appear to be real. Though they didn't dare ask if it were glass, lest they appear rude so soon into their visit. They did not want to make a bad impression on the locals. But still, that did not prevent Renard from observing the woman and wondering what made her so peculiar. She wore the poorest choice of clothing one could imagine. It really wasn’t a matter of poverty, as Renard found that even the most rural places had at least a few women quite skilled in fabrics. It wasn’t the lack of material, either. She simply must’ve chosen to wear what really resembled nothing more than just a bag with some sleeves. The fabric was harsh, as though it were made of some hay or really thick and abrasive cotton. The woman wasn’t particularly unattractive either. She just appeared as though she did not try. This bothered him greatly.

While her and Father Craner spoke, exchanging words, he looked at the home behind the woman. It was a small shed looking thing. Of course, it was a home. It was always a home, he thought. It did appear quite cozy, too. From where he stood, he thought he saw a cat in the house. Just through the window. It was perched awkwardly on some couch, watching television.

“Over on that end, you’ll find her, I believe.”

Renard had tuned into their conversation.

“Over there? Do you not know for sure? There must not be that many people here.” Craner questioned the woman.

“As I said, it’s as I believe.” She scratched her leg through her bag of a dress. “I can’t tell you nothing for sure, mister Father.”

“I’m sure of that. Just point the direction.”

“Over there, sir.”

She pointed over to a large row of houses.

“Which?”

“That one with the dog on the fencepost.”

There was a house with a dog. A sad-looking dog, with hair too long. It looked greasy, or wet. It was chained to the fencepost.

“Should they be having a dog chained up like that?” asked Renard.

The woman looked over to Renard with disdain. “I don’t know, mister,” she said mockingly. She pulled on a cigarette from a small pocket haphazardly sewn unto her dress. She lit it with a match, placing it between her lips. “And,” she said, though her words were more unintelligible as the cigarette hung from her tongue. “They let that dog out in the day. It just can’t be out in the night.”

“Why?”

“Trouble.”

“Trouble?” repeated Renard with monotone disbelief.

“Trouble.”

He looked over at the dog. It was sad— at least, it appeared to him to be.

“Right. We’ll head over.”
